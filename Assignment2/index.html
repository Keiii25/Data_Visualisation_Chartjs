Climate Change
How is it affecting the globe?

Visualising a warmer world

Temperature changes has been increasing gradually across the globe especially in least developing countries. 2020 was the second warmest year on record since 1850, followed by 2016. The global mean temperature in 2020 is estimated to have been 1.27 °C (2.29 °F) above the average temperature of the late 19th century, from 1850-1900, a period often used as a pre-industrial baseline for global temperature targets.

Global Temperature Anomaly of Land and Ocean

The earth has become warmer compared to the 1951 to 1980 average. The relative temperature is increasing gradually with no signs of decreasing back to the normal temperature anytime soon. Warmer temperatures over time are changing weather patterns and disrupting the usual balance of nature. This poses many risks to human beings and all other forms of life on Earth.

The impacts of Climate Change on Mortality

Climate change is responsible for the rise of natural disasters such as flood, wildfires, droughts, ocean acidification and etc.. All these events has disrupted the lives of people which in turns causes deaths. There is a positive correlation between the rise of temperature and number of deaths per 1000 in the world. This relationship is especially evident in Europe.

Greenhouse Gas Composition

Where does carbon dioxide came from?

Greenhouse gas is the main issue that is causing global warming as these gases get traps within the atmosphere and prevents the heat from escaping the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide is accountable for 79% of the greenhouse gases, followed by Methane, Nitrous Oxides and Flurinated Gases.
The primary source of carbon dioxide is from the burning of fossils fuels (Lindsey, 2022). The major fossils fuel in use are coal, gas and oil that is used for industry purpose or for everyday lives.

Carbon Dioxide Emission

Qatar tops the list of carbon emission per capita in the world. This is because of the high usage of fossils fuels in the country and the small population of the country.
Surprising, Australia has made it into the top 10 in the list. This is because depends heavily on fossils fuel for source of electricity and exportation. About 60% of Australia’s electricity over the past year came from burning coal thus contributing to high emissionn of carbon dioxide. Developing countries contributes to more than half of the carbon dioxide emission in the world. (Morton, 2021)
